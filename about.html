I'm Lucio Paiva, a software developer with a Computer Science Master's degree from Federal University of Rio de Janeiro. I currently live in Rio with my wife Priscila and work at Zwift, a very cool indoor cycling online game. Programming computers is my life's passion. I taught myself how to program by the age of 13 by reading a programming tutorial that came in an Electronics magazine called Microsistemas my father subscribed to. I started with Visual Studio 3, then I went to BASIC, Pascal and Delphi. Then I went reverse engineering other programs and eventually started to crack applications just for fun. I learned Intel assembly language, played a lot making my own experimental viruses and even wrote some Windows programs in pure assembly using MASM. [Iczelion's page][10] was a fantastic resource at the time and it is still online as I write this! Just amazing. I went to CEFET in 1998, a technical school in the Rio area where my father still teaches electronics as of today, where I took a technical-grade course on computer science. That's where I started to play with computer networking together with my friend Guilherme, playing a lot with BackOrifice, Netbus and started making my own "remote administration" tools. It taught me a lot about network connections, how network layers are designed and how operating systems deal with it. Fast-forward a few years, in 2003 I joined UFRJ. It's a federal university, so you have to pass a test to enter it. I was probably the first candidate at that year (2003) that knew the results before the public announcement was made. I managed to crack the password algorithm that allowed candidates to check their test grades, so I made a little tool to crawl into every candidate's accounts and download it to my own database so I could rank candidates by grade myself. I could then verify that I was at position 15 in the overall ranking, meaning I passed :-) The password was composed of 10 numeric digits, by I had only figured out the first 9. That meant I had to try up to 10 passwords for each candidate. I ended up running 10 sockets in parallel, each querying for one combination and then I recorded the right one. One year later I was able (with the help of my friend André Luiz) to crack the last digit, which was made of a series of modulus operations on the previous digits. It was also André that presented me to the best game I played so far: Ultima Online. I am a big fan of UO and my biggest joy with it was to automate my character to behave like it had a real intelligence behind it all the time. It would gather resources, move around the virtual world, buy and sell stuff, all unattended. I wrote several scripts for EasyUO, [some of them published][20], and I also started making my own macroing tool, LlamaScript. Although I never finished it, it was really fun to work on it. It would scan UO client's program memory, looking for certain assembly instructions, changing them at runtime and injecting code that would detour the game's logic to run my macroing tool's routines. Some years later I even started making my own experimental game client using Node.js and Chromium (before I knew about Electron). Speaking of games, as a child my favorite game by far was [Rome: AD 92][30]. Back in 2005 I found it among by backup stuff, played it a bit and then thought about writing to the original developer to thank him. To my surprise, not only [Steve Grand][40] replied to my email, he also gave me Rome's source code! Fast-forward to 2009, I was finishing my graduation and my final project was an electronic chromatic guitar tuner, a hardware device having a PIC at its core that was responsible for detecting the periodicity of the input signal and translate it to the 12-tone equal temperament scale, showing how many cents the input tone was off using as reference the closest known note. It was my first attempt at playing with sound. Although it only worked for instruments whose fundamental frequency was the prevalent one, it worked pretty well. A few years later, in 2012, I was finishing my Master's degree and my final thesis theme was traffic coordination and control. And I mean traffic as in vehicles, not network packets :-) It was one of my earliest desires as a programmer to be able to make a vehicle simulation system. My academic advisor, Felipe França, presented me with an algorithm he devised to solve the [dining philosopher's problem][50] with varying priorities. The algorithm, known as [Scheduling By Multiple Edge Reversal][60], was applied by me to control the phases of a traffic signal according to incoming traffic, as well as to coordinate traffic corridors and guarantee maximum road efficiency by creating so-called green corridors and optimizing average speed and travel time. The simulator had one part made in Java and a separate process (a Java applet) that showed the simulation happening. I still want to revisit this project at some point and make a web application version of it. During the period of 2011 and 2014 I co-founded a startup with some friends and most of the time there I worked doing R&D. Our first product was a groceries price comparison mobile app called BoaLista. At that time I developed an information retrieval system to match and group products from different online stores that had no barcode information, so we could properly compare product prices and enlarge our product price base. So I designed my own algorithm for it and it worked with great precision. I had to take into account things like different product sizes, weights, flavors, typos, lack of standards when defining product names and descriptions, etc... it was really challenging.
